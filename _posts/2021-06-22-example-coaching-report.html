Definition of
Resilience

Many people have encountered an adverse experience at some point in their lives. Yet, some bounce back quickly while others endure the negativity for a longer period. A distinguishing factor between these people is their level of resilience.

Resilience has been widely defined as the ability to adapt to the changing environment and to bounce back from adversity and personal setbacks. Resilience has also been recognised as a measure of stress and the ability to cope with negative events, and can thus be defined as the personal qualities that enable an individual to thrive in difficult circumstances.

Research suggests that resilient people adapt more successfully in response to major life events and traumatic experiences (Sarkar & Fletcher, 2014). Research also shows that resilience is not necessarily related to personality or intelligence (Carver & Connor-Smith, 2010).

Interpreting the Adult Resilience Scale Scores

This report is a confidential summary of the candidate’s responses to the Adult Resilience Scale, and provides descriptions to interpret the scores. The Adult Resilience Scale measures five dimensions of resilience and provides an overall Total Resilience Score. When interpreting the candidate’s results, it is important to remember that scores are neither good nor bad, and should be used as development opportunities.

Model of Resilience

The five dimensions of resilience as measured in the Adult Resilience Scale are as follows:

Threat Perception

Awareness of potential or actual threat and ability to formulate a plan.

Adjustment

Ability to adjust when unexpected events cause disruption.

Decision Making

Ability to make clear decisions during stressful situations.

Coping

Capacity to deal with stressful events and ability to return to normal routine.

Recovery

Speed of returning to normal activity after challenging situations.

Adult Resilience Scale and Bell Curve Distribution

A bell curve is the informal name of a graph that depicts a normal probability distribution. The raw scores on the Adult Resilience Scale are best viewed in the below bell curve distribution. The bell curve represents a ‘normal distribution’ of the general population. A normal distribution has the average (or mean) centred and an equal distribution above and below average like the curve below. The bell curve assumes there is an equivalent number of people above and below average, and a very small number of people two standard deviations above and below the average (mean).

The raw scores on the Adult Resilience Scale are converted to mean (average) scores and standard deviations across five intervals ranging from Very Low, Low, Average, High, and Very High. For further information on scores and interpretation, refer to the Adult Resilience Scale Manual.
